English · 7th grade · Past tense

Past Simple

one action, one form, no matter who did it!

In the present tense, the verb changes depending on who is doing the action (he plays, but I play). In Past Simple, this doesn't happen — every pronoun uses the exact same verb form.

I you we they he she it
↓ all of them use the same form ↓
played
I played football.  ·  She played football.  ·  They played football.
No -s, no -es, no extra endings for "he / she / it" like in the present tense. One form fits everyone.

Two families of verbs

Every verb in English belongs to one of two groups in the past tense — and they behave very differently.

Regular verbs

Just add -ed

Most English verbs are regular. To make the past form, add -ed to the base verb — and the spelling follows a few small rules.

  • Most verbs: add -ed → play → played
  • Verb ends in -e: add only -d → live → lived
  • Consonant + y: change y to -ied → study → studied
  • Short vowel + consonant: double the consonant → stop → stopped
play → played watch → watched clean → cleaned
Irregular verbs

No rule — they just change

Irregular verbs don't follow the -ed pattern at all. Each one has its own past form, and there's only one way to learn them: memorize each one.

go → went eat → ate see → saw have → had
